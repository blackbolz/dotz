THE DOTZ LORE

The First Dot

In the beginning, there was nothing—an endless blank expanse. Silence without shape. Emptiness without end. Then, without warning, a single dot appeared. Small, simple, insignificant. Yet in that speck was possibility itself.

The Birth of Many

The First Dot was not alone for long. It split, then multiplied. Two became four. Four became eight. Soon the blank void was no longer empty—thousands of tiny sparks, each a dot, spreading like stars across the canvas of creation. What was once a nothing became a pattern. What was once meaningless became a swarm.

The Realm of Connection

The dots did not drift without purpose. When they touched, they connected, forming lines, webs, constellations. Together, they created meaning from nothing. Roads between worlds, maps of the unseen, and constellations that guided lost travelers. To be a dot was to be small; to be DOTZ was to be endless.

The Threat of Erasure

But not all forces welcomed the swarm. From the shadows of emptiness emerged The Erasers—beings who despise pattern and seek to return everything to void. With each swipe, entire constellations vanish. Without resistance, every dot would be rubbed away, leaving only nothingness behind.

The Multiplying Rebellion

Yet DOTZ do not fear. Their strength is not in the single speck, but in the collective. When one is erased, ten more emerge. When a constellation is broken, another rises elsewhere. No hand can silence the swarm, for their nature is to multiply, to persist, to fill every void.

The March of DOTZ

Now, the blank canvas trembles beneath the endless spread of the swarm. They do not conquer with swords, nor rule with crowns. They conquer by existing, multiplying, covering every inch of the void with their stubborn, simple presence. A million specks. A billion stars. A trillion points of unstoppable growth.

And at the center of it all stands the First Dot, silent yet smiling. For it knows: one was enough.

Because in the end, nothing can stop the swarm.

We are $DOTZ.

We multiply.
